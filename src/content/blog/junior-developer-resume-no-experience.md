When you do not have full-time engineering experience yet, your resume still needs evidence. The evidence just comes
from smaller places: internships, coursework, shipped projects, open-source contributions, campus work, and support or
operations roles where you solved technical problems.

Lead with a short summary, then list the tools you can use without overstating depth. After that, give your strongest
project the same treatment you would give a job: what problem it solved, which stack you used, what you personally built,
and what changed because of it.

Avoid long lists of tutorials. A hiring team learns more from one finished project with deployment notes, tests, and a
clear README than from ten cloned apps. Include links only when the project is presentable and easy to inspect.

For junior resumes, clarity matters more than volume. Keep the format simple, make dates easy to scan, and write bullets
that prove you can learn, debug, communicate, and finish work.
